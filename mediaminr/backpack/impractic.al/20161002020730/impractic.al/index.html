Marcus Guttenplan is a designer and researcher specializing in rapidly developing prototypes tailored to emerging user needs. Considerably experienced working on interdisciplinary teams and in fast-paced environments, his in-depth knowledge of the development stack allows him to bridge gaps in the design process and

invent creative solutions to complex problems. From art installations to national security, his transmedia approach to research and ability to translate results into experimental models make him a unique asset to any creative team at any stage of the design process.

WORK HISTORY




    Area 1 Security, Product Design Engineer, 2015-2016
    Researching and designing user experience and product interfaces for network security applications for government and Fortune 500 companies. Focused on preventing highly targeted social engineering campaigns, worked with engineers and security experts to design and visualize event data at petabyte scale, creating usable solutions based on customer feedback. Developed weekly attack summary email visualization (tables, baby), helped overhaul internal dashboards, and worked on product UX. Also developed transactional marketing emails, designed transmedia collateral for anything and everything, and sourced production for the design team.
    The Tor Project, UX Contributor, 2016
    Contributed to macro conversations about Tor with other contributors from around the world once a week. Discussions center around specific flows in the product, optimizing those for international users with real threat models and a detrimental need to not make mistakes, and wrangling the beast that is Tor generally into style guides and prototypes to make eng/dev life easier for other distributed teams of contributors.
    Intel Wearable Design Studio / DSN, User Experience Researcher, 2014
    Working with Intel’s Wearable group and a teammate, we built a wearable device to steal information from other wearables in the environment. We created a hardware prototype to highlight vulnerabilities in connection and communication and demonstrated it using pickpocket techniques in a public context. Featured on Russia 1 news.
    Lab of Speculative Ethnography, Research Technologist, 2014 Together with a team from Occidental, Wesleyan, Fordham, SAIC, and Art Center we built custom documentary devices, programmatic tools, and used hands-on field research to collect data. We made a modular system of suits and sensors that embody afrofuturist perspectives. Research included community interactions throughout Los Angeles, performative fieldwork at Perform Chinatown 2014 and the American Association of Anthropologists conference.
